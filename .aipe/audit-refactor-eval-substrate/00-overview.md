# Chapter 00 — Overview

## The codebase in one paragraph

blooming insights is a single-developer Next.js 16 app that hangs four Claude agents off one Bloomreach MCP server, with no database. The agent layer is one shared `runAgentLoop` (`lib/agents/base.ts:48-176`) driving four specialists — `MonitoringAgent`, `DiagnosticAgent`, `RecommendationAgent`, `QueryAgent` — through tool calls mediated by a hand-built `McpClient` (`lib/mcp/client.ts:79-172`) with TTL cache, 1.1s spacing for the ~1 req/s Bloomreach limit, and exponential-backoff retry with parsed `Retry-After` honoring. The route layer is three NDJSON-streaming handlers (`app/api/briefing/route.ts`, `app/api/agent/route.ts`, `app/api/mcp/*`) at `maxDuration = 300` each. State lives in process-local `Map`s, an AES-256-GCM encrypted OAuth cookie, browser `sessionStorage`, and one committed JSON snapshot — there is no SQL, no Redis, no queue. 169 vitest tests cover `lib/`; zero tests cover `app/api/`.

## The staff engineer's overall take

**The load-bearing finding: the eval substrate is built but the eval harness is not, and that single gap caps every other refactor opinion in this book.**

Here is what I mean. The hard work of running an eval — the part most codebases never get around to — is already done. The NDJSON trace surface (`lib/mcp/events.ts:4-17`) is a span sequence. The replay store (`lib/state/investigations.ts:11-41`) replays it deterministically. `parseAgentJson` + type guards (`lib/mcp/validate.ts:1-57`) give you typed agent outputs to score. The scripted-Anthropic harness (31 tests across 5 agent files, anchored in `.aipe/study-testing/01-scripted-anthropic-harness.md`) proves the plumbing works end-to-end against a queued fake. The `McpCaller` interface (`lib/agents/base.ts:16-22`) lets you swap in a fake mid-loop without touching code. Five seams, all already cut. The harness is the keystone, and the keystone is the only stone missing. **That is unusual. Most L1 codebases skip the substrate too.**

Now look at what that one missing piece controls. Without a measured agreement rate against a labeled goldset, you cannot ship the cheapest perf fix in the codebase (the five lines of `res.usage` logging at `lib/agents/base.ts:102` and three peer call sites) and know whether the synthesis fallback you suspect (`lib/agents/diagnostic.ts:87-126`, `lib/agents/recommendation.ts:82-132`) is the cost concentration the perf audit names. You cannot ship the circuit breaker the system-design audit names as Ceiling 2 and know it didn't drop your accuracy by changing retry behavior. You cannot lift `synthesize()` into `runAgentLoop` (the cleanest refactor in the book — Chapter 1) and know the lift preserved the behavior that mattered. You cannot tighten `maxToolCalls` from 6 to 4 (the perf audit's lever) without a number to compare against. **Every refactor in this book is rate-limited by the same missing measurement.** That's why the through-line is eval substrate, not "fix the insights.ts race" or "extract three hooks from page.tsx" — those are correct fixes; they're just downstream of the one thing that gates whether you can know the fix worked.

What I'd be nervous about if I had to maintain this: not the architecture. The architecture is honestly aligned with what the app does today, and `study-system-design/audit.md` walks the boundaries cleanly. I'd be nervous about the **resume-vs-reality flip**: this codebase reads L2 from the outside (4 agents, MCP integration, OAuth, streaming, structured outputs) and falls back to L1 on contact (no eval, no cost meter, one documented post-mortem that's a test-infra bug, not an AI-layer bug). A senior interviewer who pulls on "how do you know any of this is good" pulls the entire claim. The fix is not to rewrite the architecture — the architecture is correct. The fix is to ship the eval harness so the claim survives the pull.

What's good and worth preserving across every refactor in this book: the `McpClient` deep module (`.aipe/study-software-design/01-mcp-client-deep-module.md`), `runAgentLoop`'s reuse across four agents with zero duplication of the loop itself, the schema-gated coverage pattern (`lib/agents/categories.ts`) that prevents the monitoring agent from spending budget on impossible categories, and the three-tier graceful degrade for non-JSON output (`tryParse → synthesize() → FALLBACK`). The three RFCs (`.aipe/rehearse-design-doc/`) defending no-database, NDJSON-not-SSE, and deterministic-supervisor-not-LLM-router are the strongest signal in the repo for "this engineer can defend a choice against the obvious alternative" — those decisions are not what needs revisiting.

The one thing I'd change if I could only change one thing: **ship the eval harness in `evals/` as spec'd in `.aipe/drills/evals-observability-induce-eval-gap-build-min-eval-harness.md`** — five files, ~5 hours, unblocks every other rung. Not a refactor in the catalog sense. The most consequential code change a refactor book can recommend, because it's the change that lets you do the other refactors with evidence.

## Reading order and chapter summaries

The book has six chapters. Each is sized for one sitting. **Read 01 first** — it carries the load-bearing finding (the `synthesize()` duplication as the cleanest worked example of why the missing eval keeps even an obvious refactor from landing safely). **Read 04 second** if you want the structural argument compressed into one chapter (the eval substrate as a discovered architectural pattern that needs one more module to complete). **Read 05 last** — it's the meta-take that holds up best after you've seen the specific takes that produced it.

### Chapter summaries

**Chapter 01 — Composition.** *Verdict: lift `synthesize()` into `runAgentLoop`; it's the cleanest refactor in the book and the one whose payoff is held back by the missing eval.* Three DEEP techniques (Extract Function on `synthesize()`, Replace Conditional with Polymorphism inside the loop's `forceFinal` branch, Inline Function on the `parseDiagnosis` / `parseRecommendations` near-twins), two BRIEF (Rename in `lib/insights/derive.ts`, Extract Variable on the synthesis-instruction boilerplate), a tail of MENTIONS. The chapter argues that every composition opinion in the book is downstream of one missing measurement — you can lift, you cannot prove the lift didn't change agent behavior.

**Chapter 02 — Structural.** *Verdict: `evals/` is the one missing structural seam in the repo. Extract `runInvestigation(deps)` is the second.* DEEP on Module Boundary (the `evals/` directory that doesn't exist), DEEP on Dependency Inversion (the route handler that imports `Anthropic` and `connectMcp` at the module top, blocking the only test that would actually catch a regression in the live wiring), DEEP on Effect Isolation (the global `Map` in `lib/state/insights.ts:4` as the canonical example of state shape that survived the no-database decision but didn't get session-scoped). BRIEF on Move Function (`insightToAnomaly` colocation). The structural chapter is where the eval through-line is most visible — every structural finding either enables or blocks the eval seam.

**Chapter 03 — Patterns.** *Verdict: this codebase already reaches for the named patterns where they earn their keep; the one pattern it's missing is Observer (the cost meter), and that's the same gap as the eval gap with a different name.* DEEP on Observer/Probe (the `res.usage` non-read at four call sites and the absent phase-timing instrumentation), DEEP on Strategy (the four agents are already a Strategy on `runAgentLoop`; `synthesize()` should be the recovery strategy on the same axis), BRIEF on Circuit Breaker (named in two audits, ~50 LOC, not blocked by eval but blocked by no measurement to validate against), BRIEF on Adapter (`McpCaller` interface as the seam — already done well, named for the pattern it instantiates), MENTIONS on State Machine (deliberately absent — the agent loop is correct without one). The pattern chapter is the shortest because the codebase has been disciplined about not reaching for patterns it doesn't need.

**Chapter 04 — DSA.** *Verdict: this codebase has no DSA-shaped issues worth a refactor. The chapter is honest and short.* One MENTION (the truncate function at `lib/agents/base.ts:31-34` is O(n) string concat, fine at 16k chars), one BRIEF on Map keyed by composite string (the `McpClient.cache` key `${name}:${JSON.stringify(args)}` is the only DSA-shaped choice in the repo and it's right). The chapter closes by naming what the codebase doesn't exercise: no graph traversal, no priority queue, no tree, no DP. The DSA portfolio in `me.md` is rich; this app doesn't reach for it because the work is I/O-bound, not algorithmic. Honest naming beats inventing.

**Chapter 05 — Principles.** *Verdict: this codebase honors SRP, Locality of Behaviour, and Principle of Least Surprise well; it strains against Dependency Inversion at the route layer and Separation of Concerns in `app/page.tsx`. The most interesting principle in this codebase is Tell-Don't-Ask — applied beautifully in `McpClient`, violated subtly by every `res.usage` call site that asks and doesn't tell.* DEEP on SRP (the eight-concerns-in-one-file pattern at `app/page.tsx`), DEEP on Dependency Inversion (the module-top `Anthropic` import that makes the route layer untestable), DEEP on Tell-Don't-Ask (the `res.usage` pattern reframed as a Tell-Don't-Ask violation — the SDK has the data, the call sites silently discard it). BRIEF on DRY, Separation of Concerns, Locality of Behaviour. NOT FOUND on Liskov Substitution (no inheritance hierarchy worth checking), Open/Closed (not a framework codebase), Interface Segregation (the `McpCaller` interface is already minimal). The chapter closes on the principle this codebase honors by default and the one it strains against most — and they're the same one viewed from two angles.

## How to use this book

Skim the chapter map at the top of each chapter. Read DEEP sections; skim BRIEF; treat MENTIONS as reference. Verdicts are the things to return to when you're deciding whether to actually do the refactor. The book is the reading; the verdicts are the reference.

The book does NOT tell you which refactor to do this week. That's `audit-cleanup`'s job (triaged action list with severity, effort, decision). The book tells you what one staff engineer would have an opinion about and why. The two work in tandem: read this book to think; consult `audit-cleanup` when you're planning the next sprint.
