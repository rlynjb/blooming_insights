# Chapter 00 — Overview

## The codebase in one paragraph

blooming insights is a single-developer Next.js 16 app where the agent stack (four Claude agents, one Bloomreach `McpClient`, three NDJSON-streaming routes) is small and focused — and the client layer is where the cognitive load piles up. The feed page (`app/page.tsx`) is 817 LOC, 15 `useState` slots, two `useEffect` blocks, and eight independent concerns at one altitude. Next to it sits `app/investigate/[id]/page.tsx` at 225 LOC, which delegates everything that isn't layout to `useInvestigation` (`lib/hooks/useInvestigation.ts`) and proves the pattern works. The two files describe the same app at two altitudes. The disparity is the subject of this notebook.

## The staff engineer's overall take

**The load-bearing finding: five seams could form on the feed page; two preconditions block any one of them landing safely — and the preconditions are not optional.**

Here's what I mean. The cleanup audit on 2026-06-14 names the page.tsx hooks-extraction as `fix-later #8`. The plan at `.aipe/specs/refactors/00-plan-2026-06-14.md` ranks it as Phase 3, behind seven `fix-now` items in Phase 1 and two preconditions in Phase 2. The two preconditions are (A) lift the NDJSON line-reader into `lib/streaming/ndjson.ts` so the extraction has a tested kernel to compose, and (B) add route-level integration tests so the extraction has a contract to verify "behaviour preserved" against. **Both preconditions are real.** Skipping A means the hooks extraction has to refactor the NDJSON loop in flight, which is exactly the "behavior-preserving slides into behavior-changing" failure mode the cleanup audit's hard rules name. Skipping B means the extraction lands with no integration coverage — the 169 unit tests prove the lib/ layer works, none of them prove the feed page still mounts, streams, and reconnects the same way it did yesterday.

The five seams the page could split into are not three. The audit names three hooks (`useBriefingStream`, `useReconnectPolicy`, `useDemoCapture`). The book argues you should land **five** seams in sequence: precondition A (the pure NDJSON kernel module) plus four hooks (`useModePersistence` as the easy first cut, then `useBriefingStream`, then `useReconnectPolicy`, then `useDemoCapture`). The audit folded `useModePersistence` into the page because at ~10 LOC it's almost too small to be its own hook — but that's the case for extracting it FIRST. It's the lowest-risk extraction (no async, no NDJSON, no reconnect), it warms up the test seam, and it gives the integration test a small win to anchor to before the harder lifts. Five seams, in order: ndjson module → mode hook → stream hook → reconnect hook → capture hook. The page collapses to ~120 LOC.

What I'd be nervous about if I had to maintain this through the extraction: not the hooks. The hooks are mechanical lifts — the seams are already in the file as logical regions (state at L95-L150, capture at L156-L256, big effect at L258-L476, JSX at L478-L817). The hard part is the reconnect-on-revoked-token branch buried inside the NDJSON event handler (`page.tsx:400-435`). It mutates sessionStorage, calls `/api/mcp/reset`, and reloads the window. It needs to survive the lift without changing semantics — and the integration test that proves it survives doesn't exist yet. **That's precondition B; that's why precondition B is not optional.** Without it you're refactoring a reconnect path with no contract.

What's good and worth preserving across every extraction in this book: the `useInvestigation` proof-of-concept (`lib/hooks/useInvestigation.ts:43-216`) is the exact shape every extracted hook should aim for — small return shape, fat hidden body, `startedRef` latch for StrictMode, parallel closure mirror for `done`-time stash, no `AbortController` because the stream IS the product. The feed page's eight concerns are not bad concerns. They're correct concerns that simply share a file scope when they shouldn't. The extraction doesn't delete behavior; it introduces depth where there is none.

The one thing I'd change if I could only change one thing: **execute precondition A first, in its own session, with its own contract test.** Not because the NDJSON kernel is the headline (it isn't — the headline is the page itself). Because once `lib/streaming/ndjson.ts` exists as a tested module, four call sites collapse to one-liners and the page extraction becomes a different shape of problem — a state-management lift, not a state-management-plus-parser lift. **The precondition changes the difficulty class of the main refactor.** That's why it goes first, and that's why doing the main refactor before it lands is the failure mode the audit keeps warning about.

## Reading order and chapter summaries

The book has six chapters. Each is sized for one sitting. **Read 02 first** — it carries the load-bearing finding (the NDJSON kernel as Extract Module is the precondition that changes the cost of every other extraction in this book). **Read 03 second** if you want the through-line on hooks-as-Strategy compressed into one chapter (the existing `useInvestigation` is the pattern; the four feed-page hooks are siblings). **Read 05 last** — it's the meta-take that holds up best after you've seen the specific takes that produced it.

### Chapter summaries

**Chapter 01 — Composition.** *Verdict: the page is a parade of Extract Function opportunities, but only one of them survives without the preconditions — the easy lift of `useModePersistence`. Every other composition fix on the feed page is held back by the same two preconditions the audit names.* DEEP on Extract Hook (the three named hooks, but `useBriefingStream` is the one the chapter walks); DEEP on Extract Variable (the inline-style objects that already exist as named constants in sibling files); BRIEF on Move Function (`stashInsights`, `readBody`, `formatCustomerCount` belong in `lib/state/stash.ts`, `lib/http/body.ts`, `lib/format/customer.ts`); BRIEF on Rename (`demoSuffix` is misnamed — it's a query-string fragment, not a suffix). The chapter argues that the audit's "three hooks" framing undercounts by two — the parser lift is its own composition move (Chapter 02) and `useModePersistence` is the warm-up extraction (this chapter).

**Chapter 02 — Structural.** *Verdict: `lib/streaming/ndjson.ts` is the load-bearing structural seam in this notebook. Until it exists, the page extraction is a state-management refactor coupled to a wire-format refactor coupled to no test scaffold — three jobs in one diff. After it lands, the page extraction is one job.* DEEP on Extract Module (`parseNdjsonStream<T>` as an async generator; four call sites collapse to `for await ... of`); DEEP on Module Boundary (the integration-test seam at `runInvestigation(deps)` that precondition B requires); BRIEF on Move Function (`monitoringState`/`monitoringSub` belong next to the `ProcessStepper` component); MENTION on Extract Module for the demo-capture flow (it's a hook in the audit's framing, but the underlying primitive — "walk a list of insights, run an agent step per item, post the trace" — is an orchestration module that belongs in `lib/dev/capture.ts`). The structural chapter is where the through-line is most visible: every structural finding either enables a hook extraction or is the seam that catches the next reconnect bug.

**Chapter 03 — Patterns.** *Verdict: the codebase has already reached for the Custom Hook pattern once (`useInvestigation`) and reached well. The four extractions on the feed page are not new patterns — they are the same pattern, applied to a sibling component. The most useful framing in this chapter is Hook-as-Strategy.* DEEP on Custom Hook (the existing `useInvestigation` as the exemplar, the four feed-page hooks as siblings, the contract test that makes them interchangeable); BRIEF on Strategy (the mode toggle reframed — demo and live are two strategies on one stream URL; today the page branches on a string, tomorrow it could be a `BriefingSource` strategy if a third mode ever ships); MENTION on State Machine (deliberately absent — the feed page's status field is a tagged union of four values, which is a state machine but doesn't earn its own type); NOT FOUND on Adapter (the `McpCaller` interface is in the lib layer, not the client layer). The shortest chapter, because the codebase has been disciplined about not reaching for patterns it doesn't need on the client side.

**Chapter 04 — DSA.** *Verdict: the page has no DSA-shaped issues worth a refactor. The chapter is honest and short.* One BRIEF on the trace-item state shape (currently a flat array with O(n) replace-last-by-toolName at `page.tsx:368-385`; fine at ~30 items, would be a Map<toolName, RunningTool[]> at ~1000 items, which this app will never reach). One MENTION on the `setCoverage((prev) => prev.some(...) ? prev : [...prev, evt.item])` dedup at `page.tsx:335-337` (O(n) per insert is correct at 10 categories). The 14 useState slots are not a DSA problem; they're an SRP problem (Chapter 05). The chapter closes by naming what the page doesn't exercise: no graph traversal, no priority queue, no tree, no DP. The page is event-driven state accumulation — list appends, last-element-replace, single-field set. Boring data-structure work, correctly done. Honest naming beats inventing.

**Chapter 05 — Principles.** *Verdict: the page violates SRP loudly and Separation of Concerns subtly. Honors Locality of Behaviour better than it gets credit for. Strains hardest against the Principle of Least Surprise at the reconnect branch — and that's the one the integration test from precondition B has to catch.* DEEP on Single Responsibility (the eight-concerns-in-one-file pattern, reframed from the eval-substrate notebook to anchor on the depth ratio, not the LOC count); DEEP on Separation of Concerns (the reconnect-on-revoked-token branch buried inside the NDJSON `error` handler — wire-format concern in the same switch as session-state concern); DEEP on Principle of Least Surprise (the surprise the next reader will hit: a `case 'error'` arm that calls `/api/mcp/reset` and reloads the window — that's an effect inside a parser); BRIEF on Locality of Behaviour (the page's comments do honor LoB; the reconnect branch is well-commented, just badly placed); MENTION on Tell-Don't-Ask (one minor case: the page reads `localStorage.getItem('bi:mode')` to decide URL; would be cleaner if mode told URL via a getter); NOT FOUND on Liskov, Open/Closed, Interface Segregation, Dependency Inversion (none of the agent-layer principle violations from the eval-substrate notebook bite at the client layer). The chapter closes on the principle this page honors by default (LoB) and the one it strains against most (POLS at the reconnect arm) — and they're the same comment, viewed from two angles.

## How to use this book

Skim the chapter map at the top of each chapter. Read DEEP sections; skim BRIEF; treat MENTIONS as reference. Verdicts are the things to return to when you're deciding whether to actually do the extraction. The book is the reading; the verdicts are the reference.

The book does NOT tell you which extraction to do this week. That's `audit-cleanup`'s job (already done: `fix-later #8`, with the two preconditions named). The book tells you what one staff engineer would have an opinion about and why, with one explicit ranking: **precondition A before any hook lift, precondition B before any hook lift, `useModePersistence` before `useBriefingStream`, `useBriefingStream` before the other two.** Five seams. That's the order. The rest is opinion.
